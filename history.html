History

A historical overview of March Madness

The Start.

The first NCAA Division I (that's one for all you non-Romans ) was played in 1939. The first winner of the Tournamnet was the Oregon Ducks, who, with a record of 29-5, beat THE Ohio State University 46-33 in the inagural championship game.

The Expansion.

The 1939 tournament had just eight teams. The field doubled to 16 in 1951 and continued its slow but sure expansion until 1985 where the modern 64-team format was established. These dates are important to note because some fans believe that any championship won before 1985 is lesser than because the tournament was smaller. I am definitely 100% not some fans, no way. The tournament continued its expansion when the Mountain West Conference joined Division I and the total team amount increased once again to 65. A single, what is called play-in, game was added prior to the first round. In 2011, three more teams were added to round out the number to a nice even 68, and thus three more games in the play-in round which rounded out the First Four. These First Four games are played on Tuesday and Wednesday and the team must win that game in order to progress further into the tournament.

The Phrase.

The first ever use of the term (March Madness) is credited to an Illinois high school official named Henry V. Porter who began using it in 1939. The term didn't really become commonplace in the NCAA Tournament until it was used by CBS Broadcaster Brent Musburger in the coverage of the 1982 tournament. Since then, the term has become synonymous with the NCAA tournament. The term adds a bit of mystique to the tournament, with all fans alike thinking the same thing, "It's March anything can happen."

The Song

If you were thinking: "Man basketball itself isn't the most entertaining I could really use a song to capture my attention then boy do I have news for you." At the end of the tournament a song called One shining Moment is played accompanied by a montage of the best moments of March Madness. Every buzzer beater, every upset, every crying or screaming fan is displayed in all of its glory. This anthem was first used for the NCAA tournament in 1987 and has become a staple of the tournament ever since then. I will never admit to crying to a song but if there is ever a song that would make me cry it would be that one. You can watch the latest version of the song (the 2023 version) here.
